Transitioning to OsloMet

Student one went right from upper-secondary school to university and experienced a change in the expected degree of independent work. He had previously dabbled with JavaScript, C and HTML, so this semester served as a useful refresher with regards to coding and programming. This semester has nonetheless provided a useful foundation and introduction to object-oriented programming.

Student two pursued a professional degree in psychology at the University of Oslo, but decided to abandon it and instead enter the workforce. For five years he worked in retail as a service technician of consumer electronics, but honestly didn’t feel like the position provided enough challenges. He also realized the limited career opportunities in the sector, and decided to pursue the field of technology. In the year leading up to the beginning of this first semester, he enrolled in CS50, Harvard’s introductory computer science course, which introduced many of the key concepts used in coding and programming. He felt well prepared, though a bit nervous, to begin his journey at OsloMet.

Student three went right from taking subjects on his own to OsloMet, and was therefore used to working independently. He experienced challenges with allocating his time and spending that time working efficiently, but eventually adjusted to student life. He had little previous coding experience, but found learning the subjects of this semester to be easily manageable.

Learning coding and programming

Learning to code and program has been quite enjoyable, because we all have a deep-seated interest for technology in one way or another. We all had different levels of coding experience, and due to the digitization of everything school related, we could progress through each subject at our own pace. If we would like, the Canvas video player allows us to increase the playback speed of pre-recorded lectures, which is especially useful when you’re already familiar with the subject of the module.

Fortunately, the foundation of HTML and CSS is not that difficult, but we’ve faced challenges when creating responsive layouts, and the issues tend to appear unexpectedly. For instance, we had to adapt to images scaling incorrectly. Sometimes, the text in our website’s paragraph shrunk on smaller displays to a level of illegibility. The paragraphs themselves would also narrow, and the resulting lines of text would become impractically short. To reduce the necessity for several size and scale relevant CSS rules, we’ve learned to use the clamp() CSS function to set lower and upper limits.

Learning GitHub this semester has been one of our most valuable experiences. Using GitHub to work together on collaborative projects has presented its own challenges. After developing code simultaneously we encountered conflicts. We’ve had to remind ourselves to update our repositories frequently. Alternatively, we could work on multiple branches, but to prevent having to merge each branch afterwards and to minimize divergence of our code, primarily modifying the main branch is a trade-off we’re willing to make. Especially since the participants in our group haven’t been able to meet in person as much as we’d like, the user-friendly GitHub Desktop application has proven essential in both our learning to code and exercising our coding skills.
