Accessibility

Automated test

Using Achecker (Inclusive Design Research Centre, 2021), we tested our code for potential issues and accessibility after our first draft, and adjusted accordingly. In index.html we needed to specify the lang attribute as en. We added the alt attribute to one image in learning.html which didn’t have it. We had used the header element twice in topical.html and removed one of them. Those were the only known issues we encountered, so we decided to review the suggestions for potential problems. We were told to add the dir element to identify the text direction in our navigation bars, but the element was depreeciated in HTML 4 and is no longer supported by major browsers. Next we added “skip to content” links as suggested. We double checked that all related link groups were marked as ul elements, and that they appeared in the same order across all our sites. To make the links more descriptive, we changed the link from “topical” to the actual subject of our essay. We were told that img elements may require long descriptions and lengthened all captions, and at the same time made sure that all meaningful information from the image was included. We expanded the alt attribute to include all important text conveyed in the image as suggested.

Further considerations

Beyond consulting with the automated test, to ensure our site’s accessibility right from the start of our development process, we first examined the concepts of page structure from w3.org (Web Accessibility Initiative, 2019), as well as w3’s accessibility principles (Web Accessibility Initiative, 2021). We designed our site in accordance with these principles to the best of our ability. Our accessibility related priority was to ensure full keyboard access for the end user, since we believe that will have the biggest impact for those who use screen readers or other assistive technologies. Our site accounts for low resolution displays and cognitive or specific learning disabilities such as dyslexia by exclusively using sans-serif fonts. We improved image responsiveness by using the img HTML element with its srcset attribute. We rendered and stored images in two different resolutions to account for small displays and low bandwidth. Another consideration is the simplicity of our site. We believe accounting for unique sensitivities is important, and it includes recognizing those with attention deficit disorder and other neurodevelopmental disorders. Appropriately designing our site means removing potentially interfering website elements, or anything that could cause unnecessarily overwhelming sensory stimuli, so we made sure to avoid very bright layouts or flashing colors. We believe it’s important that our site is comfortable to use for everybody, including those with photosensitivity/hypersensitivity.
